Context

A 20-year-old system running
critical public infrastructure.

Sicalwin is the accounting platform used by hundreds of Spanish municipalities to manage public budgets, execute spending, track transactions, and generate the reports required by law. It had been running for over two decades on a Visual Basic architecture functional, deeply embedded in institutional workflows, and completely unfit for what municipalities needed in 2023.

The system worked. That was the problem. The technical debt had become a compliance risk new regulations required capabilities the system simply couldn't support.

"The hardest part wasn't designing the new system. It was convincing institutions that had used the old one for 20 years that change was worth the risk."

03
The challenge
Three hard problems
that had to be solved together.

Three hard problems
that had to be solved together.

Convincing municipalities

Every municipality that switched was betting their year-end close on a new system. We had to earn trust incrementally, starting with the most willing to co-build with us.

Prioritising the right workflows

Deciding which features to rebuild first, which to defer, and which to drop entirely was the most strategically consequential work on this project.

Backward compatibility

Municipalities couldn't stop operating while migrating. The new system had to run alongside the old one and maintain continuity of legal reporting throughout.

Compliance by design

Audit readiness, role-based access, and specific reporting formats had to be foundational not afterthoughts. They shaped every data model decision.

04
How I worked
Designing with the people
who run public money.

Designing with the people
who run public money.

This was a project where the hardest decisions were not about UI. They were about what to build first, what to ask the institution to accept, and when to push and when to wait. I was Head of Product and Design, which in this context meant being the person who decided which features made it into the pilot and which didn't and defending those decisions to leadership at Berger-Levrault while keeping the Diputació de Girona willing to keep co-building.

Working in public sector requires a different kind of research. Users are not product-savvy early adopters they are civil servants with legal accountability, decades of institutional memory, and zero tolerance for disruption. I adapted the team's working method to match that: slower validation cycles, more observation, more time in the room with the actual users before touching any interface.

Our pilot programme was anchored at the Diputació de Girona. I structured it as a progressive commitment: each municipality that onboarded successfully reduced the institutional risk for the next one. That framing was as much a stakeholder management strategy as a product one.

"Every municipality had the same legal obligations but a completely different way of working. The product had to be consistent enough to be learnable and flexible enough to be real."

05
The critical decision
Don't wait for done.
Ship and migrate in parallel.

The client wanted to wait. We convinced them not to.

The original position from the Diputació de Girona was clear: no municipality would migrate until the entire platform was finished. That felt safe to them. In practice it meant we would have spent over a year building in the dark, with no real user feedback, no institutional buy-in, and no proof that what we were building actually worked in production.

"Building for a year without real users in a system this critical would have been the most dangerous thing we could do. We needed municipalities using it before it was finished."

The resistance came from two places. Institutionally, public sector clients are risk-averse by design. Personally, older civil servants had spent decades with Sicalwin and had no interest in learning something new. The younger ones were frustrated with the old system and eager for change.

We used that divide as the entry point. The strategy was to start with the workflows that younger users touched most, prove the new system worked better on those specific tasks, and let that evidence do the persuasion work with leadership. Once the first municipality closed their month-end using Wecitio and reported no issues, the conversation changed completely.

Running both systems in parallel was technically demanding for the engineering team but it was the right call. It meant municipalities never faced a hard cutover. They migrated gradually, at their own pace, with a fallback always available. That safety net was what made the risk acceptable to institutional leadership.

08
Outcome
What the migration
actually produced.

A platform municipalities
were willing to bet their year on.

The measure of success in a migration like this isn't adoption numbers it's trust. Every municipality that switched was betting their legal reporting obligations on a system that hadn't existed two years before. That none of them had to go back to Sicalwin is the outcome that matters most.

The pilot programme with the Diputació de Girona became a repeatable model. Starting with the most willing municipalities, proving the platform worked, then expanding progressively close to a hundred municipalities onboarded during my tenure, with the compliance and audit trail requirements met at every step.

09
Learnings

What institutional design
teaches you.

01
Trust is the product
In public sector, institutional trust isn't a nice-to-have it's the prerequisite for adoption. The pilot strategy wasn't just a migration plan. Every successful migration made the next one easier.
02
Compliance is architecture, not a feature
Role-based access, immutable audit trails, structured data models for legal reporting these couldn't be layered on later. The most important UX decisions happen at the data architecture level, not the interface level.
03
The most important decision is what you choose not to show
Information density that felt comprehensive to power users was cognitive overload for administrators doing repetitive tasks. Ruthless subtraction was one of the highest-value design moves on this project.
