01 Context

A 20-year-old system
running critical public infrastructure.

Sicalwin is the accounting platform used by hundreds of Spanish municipalities to manage public budgets, execute spending, track transactions, and generate the reports required by law. It had been running for over two decades on a Visual Basic architecture — functional, deeply embedded in institutional workflows, and completely unfit for what municipalities needed in 2023.

The system worked. That was the problem. Because it worked, nobody had replaced it. And because nobody had replaced it, the codebase had calcified around workarounds, the UX had never been designed intentionally, and the technical debt had become a compliance risk. New regulations required capabilities the system simply couldn't support.

"The hardest part wasn't designing the new system. It was convincing institutions that had used the old one for 20 years that change was worth the risk."

I joined as Head of Product and Design to lead the transition: defining the product vision for Wecitio — the new platform — and building the strategy to migrate municipalities without disrupting the operations that kept cities running.

02 The starting point

What we were replacing
and why it couldn't stay.

The legacy system was a dense, menu-driven application with hundreds of nested options, no visual hierarchy, and a form-based interaction model that required deep institutional knowledge to operate. New staff took months to onboard. Errors were common and hard to trace. There was no audit trail that regulators could easily interpret.

The compliance risk was the forcing function. Spanish public accounting law was evolving, and the legacy system couldn't generate the new required reports without manual intervention. That's what opened the door to modernisation.

03 The challenge

Three hard problems
that had to be solved together.

This wasn't a typical product redesign. The constraints were institutional, technical, and political at the same time. Getting any one of them wrong would have stalled the whole programme.

Convincing municipalities to migrate

Public institutions don't take technology risk lightly. Every municipality that switched was betting their year-end close on a new system. We had to earn that trust incrementally, starting with the municipalities most willing to co-build with us.

Deciding what to modernise first

The legacy system had hundreds of features. We couldn't rebuild everything at once. The roadmap decision — which workflows to prioritise, which to defer, which to drop entirely — was the most strategically consequential work I did on this project.

Backward compatibility during migration

Municipalities couldn't stop operating while they migrated. The new system had to run alongside the old one, import historical data accurately, and maintain continuity of reporting obligations throughout.

Compliance by design

Public accounting in Spain operates under strict regulatory frameworks. Every design decision had to support audit readiness, role-based access control, and the specific reporting formats required by law — not as an afterthought, but as a foundation.

04 How I worked

Field research, co-building,
and ruthless prioritisation.

I drove continuous discovery with municipalities through interviews, field visits, and structured feedback loops. This wasn't optional — the domain was too specialised for assumptions. The people who used Sicalwin every day understood compliance workflows in ways that no desk research could replicate.

I was hands-on throughout: leading co-design sessions with partner municipalities, facilitating workshops with the engineering team to align on architecture decisions, and making the key UX calls myself. As Director, I set the product vision and the design direction — but I stayed close to the work, not above it.

The roadmap was my most important deliverable. I combined regulatory requirements, customer adoption risk, and technical feasibility to sequence the build. Core budgeting and transaction workflows came first — the non-negotiables. Insights and analytics came later, once the data foundation was solid enough to support them.

Municipal field research

Interviews and site visits with interventors and finance teams to understand real workflows — not just what they said they needed, but how they actually operated day to day.

Co-building with pilot municipalities

Selected partner municipalities tested early versions in production conditions. Their feedback directly shaped the roadmap and the UX of core workflows.

Regulatory-driven prioritisation

I mapped every feature against compliance requirements to distinguish what was legally necessary from what was operationally convenient. That distinction drove the sequence of the build.

Hands-on design leadership

I led co-design sessions, ran critiques, defined the interaction patterns for complex financial workflows, and made the key design decisions myself. Direction-setting and hands-on craft, not one or the other.

05 The product

What Wecitio became.

Wecitio is a modern municipal accounting platform built around four core workflows: budget management, transaction tracking, financial reporting, and advanced insights. Each was designed with the specific needs of public-sector users — role-based access, audit trails, compliance-first data structures, and the ability to handle the complexity of Spanish municipal accounting without hiding it behind false simplicity.

06 Outcome

What the modernisation
made possible.

Wecitio established the foundation for a scalable, secure, and maintainable municipal accounting platform. The phased migration approach — co-built with pilot municipalities — reduced adoption risk and created genuine buy-in from the institutions that would use it.

The shift from a Visual Basic monolith to a modular, API-first architecture meant that new regulatory requirements could now be addressed in weeks rather than months. Audit readiness, which had previously required days of manual report preparation, became a real-time capability built into the platform from the ground up.

Most importantly, the platform changed how municipalities could think about their own financial data. Moving from a system that stored transactions to one that surfaced insights gave finance teams the ability to make decisions proactively rather than reactively.

07 Learnings

What this project
taught me about change.

  • 1

    Trust is the first product you build in public sector

    No municipality was going to migrate because the new system looked better. They migrated because we showed up consistently, listened to their concerns, involved them in the design, and proved we understood the consequences of getting it wrong. The co-building model with pilot municipalities wasn't just a research method — it was a trust-building strategy.

  • 2

    Roadmap decisions are product decisions

    Deciding what to build first in a legacy modernisation is the hardest design problem on the project. Every feature you defer is a municipality that can't fully migrate yet. I learned to be much more explicit about sequencing criteria: compliance necessity, adoption dependency, and migration risk. Without a clear framework, the backlog becomes a political negotiation rather than a strategic plan.

  • 3

    Simplicity in complex domains requires deep understanding

    The temptation in a domain as complex as public accounting is to expose the complexity and let users navigate it. The better answer is to understand it deeply enough to hide it where it doesn't need to be seen. That requires more research, more domain knowledge, and more willingness to push back on "but that's how it's always worked" as a design rationale.
