A new category of product
for a new kind of company.

Paid.ai is building monetisation infrastructure for AI-native companies: the companies that run AI agents on behalf of customers and need to track what those agents do, what it costs, and what value they create. A product category that barely existed before 2024.

The founding team had a clear thesis: AI agents do more work, cost more to run, and create more value than traditional SaaS, yet most companies building with AI have zero visibility into any of it. Which customer generates the most AI cost? Which workflow delivers the most value? Are we making money?

"The hardest part was not designing the interface. It was figuring out what the product actually was, and making something abstract and technical feel concrete and usable."

They brought me in to design the full product experience: how AI operators would define workflows, observe executions, understand the value generated, and turn that value into customer invoices and delivery reports.

02
The problem
Three hard things that had
to become one coherent system

Three hard things
that had to become one.

The product had to solve three distinct problems simultaneously, for users with very different mental models. Making them feel like one coherent system, not three tools bolted together, was the central design challenge.

01
Observability
AI operators need to see every execution, every tool call, every outcome. The risk: making the product feel like a log viewer rather than a business tool.
02
Value attribution
Translating AI executions into business value is conceptually new. No established UX pattern existed to borrow from.
03
Billing and proof
AI companies need to charge by outcomes or value delivered, and prove that value to skeptical customers. The invoice alone is not enough.
04
Coherence
Each problem had its own user, data model, and interaction pattern. The information architecture was everything.

The solution I designed was a loop: define what a workflow does, observe executions, attribute value to each outcome, generate billing and delivery reports. The loop is the product.

Research
Understanding the problem
before touching any UI

Understanding the problem
before touching any UI.

Paid.ai gave me a brief and product hypotheses. My first job was to stress-test them. I spent time with the founding team understanding their target customers: small AI companies, 2 to 10 people, building vertical agents for enterprise clients. Companies already in production with no visibility into what their agents cost or delivered.

The key insight: these companies had three completely different conversations at once. Technical conversations with engineers about reliability. Commercial conversations with customers about ROI. Internal conversations about unit economics. Most used a mix of spreadsheets, Stripe, and custom dashboards.

"The brief said billing platform. The research said: your customers do not have a billing problem. They have a value visibility problem. Billing is the last step, not the first."

Finding 01
Visibility before billing
Companies could not bill for AI value they could not see. Observability was the first problem to solve, not invoicing.
Finding 02
Evidence is the product
The delivery report with linked evidence traces was more valuable than the invoice itself.
Finding 03
Outcome is the unit
"Ticket resolved" not "12,000 tokens consumed". The atomic unit had to be human-readable.

The home dashboard answers the question that matters to an AI operator: am I making money from this? I designed it around four headline metrics, a value summary, a revenue trend, and a customer table with TCV, LTV, agents, and signals per account.

The operator home — the first view that answers "how is the business doing?" Revenue trend, value generated across all AI workflows, and a customer table showing contract value, lifetime value, agent count, and upcoming invoices.

Defining what an AI agent

The workflow is the unit of everything in Paid.ai. I designed creation as a two-step process: first define the metadata (name, customer, outcome, estimated value), then build the flow in the block-based builder. The key decision: surfacing value and billing eligibility in the builder header at all times.

The Workflows view — every AI workflow the operator has defined, the customer it runs for, the primary outcome it produces, and how much value it has generated. Each row is a business contract between the product and the customer.

Left: defining a new workflow — the operator names it, assigns a customer, selects the primary outcome, and estimates the value per execution. Right: the builder in its empty state, ready to receive blocks.

The block-based builder in its published state. The workflow header always shows the customer, outcome, value per execution, and billing eligibility — keeping business context visible while building.

Executions

Every agent run, traced
and valued in real time.

The executions view is where observability meets value attribution. Every agent run is captured: duration, cost, outcome, and value generated. The slide-in detail panel gives technical depth without cluttering the default view.

Every time an AI agent runs, Paid.ai captures the full execution — which workflow, which customer, whether it succeeded, how long it took, what it cost, what outcome it produced, and what value it generated. This is the observability layer.

Left: clicking into an execution reveals the full detail — workflow, customer, outcome, value, cost, and tabs for Trace, Logs, and Evidence. Right: the executions view in its MVP form, optimised for teams just getting started with the product.

The evidence block inside an execution detail: proof that the AI delivered what it promised, with linked execution traces, API responses, and source data. Exportable as PDF for customer reporting.

07
Value
From execution data
to business intelligence

From execution data
to business intelligence.

The value view is the strategic layer. Where executions give operational visibility, value gives business intelligence: how much has your AI generated, which workflows are performing best, which customers are getting the most out of it.

The Value view turns execution data into business intelligence — total value generated, value over time, which workflows deliver most, which customers benefit most. This is what the operator uses to understand pricing, prioritize workflows, and build the case for renewal.

The MVP version of the Value view: a focused summary of recent value events per workflow, without the full chart layer. Enough for early operators to answer "is this working?" without overwhelming them with data they can't yet act on.

Credits: the cost side of the equation. Tracking what each AI workflow consumes lets operators understand their unit economics and set sustainable pricing against the value they generate.

Billing as a value-first flow: the table shows value generated per customer before showing what to bill. The delivery report is not just an invoice but proof: executions, outcomes, value, and supporting evidence including execution traces, API responses, and source links.

The Billing view leads with value generated — operators see what was earned before deciding what to invoice. Bills move through a lifecycle: draft, ready for review, approved, exported. Value and billing status side by side.

A bill in detail: line items generated from value events, each traceable back to the execution that produced it. The breakdown makes the invoice auditable and defensible to the customer.

Left: creating a bill from AI-generated value — the operator selects the customer, period, and line item type. The draft preview updates in real time. Right: generating a delivery report — the document that proves to the customer what was delivered and what it was worth.

The delivery report: not just an invoice, but proof. Executions, outcomes, value attributed, and linked evidence — everything the customer needs to validate what they're paying for and justify renewal internally.

The Reports hub generates delivery reports per customer and period. Below the table, automated insights surface patterns the operator would otherwise have to look for — which workflows are growing, which customers have unbilled value, where success rates are dropping.

09
Two scopes
Designing for now and
for what comes next

Designing for now
and for what comes next.

I delivered two design scopes. The MVP covered the core loop: everything a team of two could ship to early customers quickly. The large product vision extended this into a full platform. The design system was consistent across both.

MVP scope
  • Workflow definition with outcome and value estimation
  • Execution list with status, cost, and value per run
  • Value view with recent value events
  • Billing table with draft bill creation
  • Basic delivery report generation
Large product vision
  • Home dashboard with revenue trends and customer table
  • Full workflow builder with block library
  • Executions with trace, logs, and evidence tabs
  • Value by workflow and customer with charts
  • Credits and observability system
  • Reports with automated insights

Workflows: MVP vs large vision. Same core model — workflows mapped to customers and outcomes — but the large scope adds paused and draft states, higher execution volumes, and richer per-row data.

Executions: MVP vs large vision. The MVP captures the essential run data. The full scope adds value attribution per run, outcome tagging, and the detail panel with trace and evidence tabs.

Value: MVP vs large vision. The MVP answers the core question — is the AI generating value? — with a focused event list. The large scope adds breakdowns by workflow and customer, and charts that show value over time.

Billing: MVP vs large vision. The MVP gets a bill out of the door. The large scope makes billing a strategic view — value earned, lifecycle tracking, and direct links to the delivery report that closes the loop with the customer.

10
Learnings
What designing a new
category teaches you

What designing a new
category teaches you.

01
The mental model is the product
In an established category, users arrive with a mental model. In a new category, you have to design both the interface and the concept. The loop was not just a UX pattern — it was the core concept the product needed to teach. Every screen had to reinforce it.
02
Abstract concepts need concrete anchors
"Value generated by AI" is meaningless without a number, an outcome, and evidence. I learned to always ask: what is the most concrete version of this? A ticket resolved, a lead qualified, a document processed. The design had to translate technical events into business language at every level.
03
Scope discipline is a design skill
Designing MVP and large scope simultaneously is a trap if you do not hold the line. I treated the two scopes as separate deliverables with separate constraints: the MVP had to be something two engineers could build in weeks, not months.
