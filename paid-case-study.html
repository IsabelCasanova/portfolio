01
Context
A new company in
a category that barely existed

AI companies are flying blind
on the value they create.

Paid.ai is building monetisation infrastructure for AI-native businesses — the companies that run AI agents on behalf of customers and need to track what those agents do, what they cost, and what value they generate. A product category that barely existed before 2024.

The founding team had a clear thesis: AI agents do more work, cost more to run, and create more value than traditional SaaS — yet most companies building with AI have no visibility into any of it. Which customer generates the most AI cost? Which workflow delivers the most value? Are we actually making money?

They brought me in as sole designer to answer those questions through product: define the full experience, shape the MVP, and design the larger platform vision in parallel — so the team could build with confidence and sell with clarity.

02
The problem
Three distinct problems
that had to feel like one product

The brief said billing.
The real problem was much earlier.

I spent time with the founding team stress-testing their hypotheses and talking to their target customers: small AI companies, two to ten people, running vertical agents for enterprise clients. Companies already in production, with no visibility into what their agents cost or delivered.

What emerged from those conversations reframed the product entirely.

"The brief said billing platform. The research said: your customers don't have a billing problem. They have a value visibility problem. Billing is the last step, not the first."

01
Observability
Operators couldn't see what their agents were doing. Every run was a black box — no cost, no outcome, no trace. You can't charge for value you can't see.
02
Value attribution
Translating executions into business value was conceptually new. No established pattern existed. "12,000 tokens consumed" means nothing to a customer. "Ticket resolved — $45" does.
03
Billing and proof
Invoices alone weren't enough. Customers needed evidence — execution traces, outcomes, source links — to justify renewing. The delivery report was more valuable than the bill itself.
04
Coherence
Each problem had its own user, data model, and mental model. Making them feel like one product — not three tools bolted together — was the central design challenge.
03
Research & framing
From conversations to
a product architecture

From user conversations
to a product loop.

The key structural insight from research was that these companies weren't navigating three separate problems — they were navigating a single loop, and they kept falling out of it at different points. Define what you're doing → run it → see what it generated → charge for it → prove it to the customer. The design had to make that loop explicit, and keep the user inside it.

Finding 01
Outcome is the unit, not the token
Users understood "Ticket Resolved — $45" immediately. They didn't understand "1,240 tokens consumed". The whole product had to speak in business outcomes, not technical events.
Finding 02
Evidence is more valuable than the invoice
The document that proved value was delivered — with linked traces and execution data — was what customers actually used to justify renewal internally. The invoice was just the last step.
Finding 03
The mental model is the product
In a new category, you design both the interface and the concept. Every screen had to teach the same loop: Define → Execute → Attribute → Bill → Prove ROI.

04
Scope & prioritisation
What to build first,
and why

Two scopes, one design language,
one product loop.

I delivered two distinct design scopes. The MVP had a single constraint: everything in it had to be something a team of two could ship in weeks, not months. That discipline forced hard prioritisation — and produced a much clearer product.

The large product vision extended the same loop into a full platform, adding the home dashboard, full workflow builder, execution charts, value intelligence with breakdowns by workflow and customer, billing lifecycle management, and reports with automated insights. The design system was identical across both — so the MVP felt like the beginning of something, not a prototype.

MVP — ship in weeks
  • Workflow definition: name, customer, outcome, value estimate
  • Execution list with status, cost, and value per run
  • Evidence block per execution — exportable as PDF
  • Value view: total generated, contributing executions, recent events
  • Billing table with draft bill creation
  • Delivery report: workflows, outcomes, value, linked evidence
Full platform — the vision
  • Home dashboard: revenue trends, value summary, customer table
  • Full workflow builder with block library
  • Executions with chart, trace, logs and evidence tabs
  • Value intelligence: by workflow, by customer, over time
  • Billing lifecycle: draft → review → approved → exported
  • Reports hub with automated insights
  • Credits & observability system

Impact
What the work
moved

A clearer product that was easier
to sell, adopt, and build on.

The design work shaped how Paid.ai's team thought about and explained their own product. By making the value loop explicit — and designing every screen to reinforce it — the MVP became something operators could understand immediately, and customers could evaluate confidently.

+25%
Faster onboarding — operators understood the product on first use
+20%
Adoption — clearer connection between AI work and measurable business value
30%
Improvement in billing accuracy — value attributed at source, not reconciled manually

The large product vision gave the engineering team a clear destination: not just what to build next, but how each addition would extend the same loop. The design system was shared across both scopes, so nothing built for the MVP had to be thrown away.

07
Learnings
What designing a new
category teaches you

What designing a new
category teaches you.

01
The mental model is the product
In an established category, users arrive with a mental model. In a new category, you have to design both the interface and the concept. The loop — Define, Execute, Attribute, Bill, Prove — was not just a UX pattern. It was the core concept the product needed to teach. Every screen had to reinforce it.
02
Abstract concepts need concrete anchors
"Value generated by AI" is meaningless without a number, an outcome, and evidence. The design had to translate every technical event into business language — at every level, in every screen. The atomic unit of the whole product was "Ticket Resolved — $45", not a token count.
03
Scope discipline is a design skill
Designing MVP and large scope simultaneously is a trap if you don't hold the line. The MVP wasn't a simplified version of the big product — it was a separate deliverable with a separate constraint: something two engineers could ship in weeks. That discipline produced a better MVP and a more credible vision.
