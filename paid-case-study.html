01
Context
A new company in
a category that barely existed.

AI companies are flying blind
on the value they create.

Paid.ai is building monetisation infrastructure for AI-native businesses the companies that run AI agents on behalf of customers and need to track what those agents do, what they cost, and what value they generate.

The founding team's thesis: AI agents do more work, cost more to run, and create more value than traditional SaaS yet most companies building with AI have no visibility into any of it. They brought me in as sole designer to define the full experience, shape the MVP, and design the larger platform vision in parallel.

02
The problem
The brief said billing.
The real problem was earlier.

The brief said billing.
The real problem was much earlier.

Time with the founding team and their target customers: small AI companies, two to ten people, running vertical agents for enterprise clients. Already in production, with no visibility into what their agents cost or delivered.

"The brief said billing platform. The research said: your customers don't have a billing problem. They have a value visibility problem. Billing is the last step, not the first."

Observability

Operators couldn't see what their agents were doing. Every run was a black box no cost, no outcome, no trace.

Value attribution

"12,000 tokens consumed" means nothing to a customer. "Ticket resolved $45" does. Translating executions into business value was conceptually new.

Billing and proof

Invoices alone weren't enough. The delivery report with execution traces, outcomes, source links was more valuable than the bill itself.

Coherence

Each problem had its own user, data model, and mental model. Making them feel like one product was the central design challenge.

03
Research & framing
From conversations
to a product loop.

From user conversations
to a product loop.

The key structural insight: these companies weren't navigating three separate problems they were navigating a single loop, falling out of it at different points.

Finding 01
Outcome is the unit, not the token
Users understood "Ticket Resolved $45" immediately. The whole product had to speak in business outcomes, not technical events, at every level and in every screen.
Finding 02
Evidence is more valuable than the invoice
The document proving value was delivered with linked traces was what customers used to justify renewal internally. The invoice was just the last step.
Finding 03
The mental model is the product
In a new category, you design both the interface and the concept. Every screen had to teach the same loop: Define, Execute, Attribute, Bill, Prove ROI.

04
Design leadership
Translating technical
thinking into product.

My job was to make a technical product legible to the people who would buy and use it.

The founding team were strong engineers with a sharp thesis. What they needed was someone who could sit in conversations with customers and prospects, absorb what the team understood deeply, and turn it into something a non-technical operator could navigate on first use.

That translation work happened at every level. In early sessions with prospects, I listened for the moment people stopped following and worked backwards from there. The phrase "12,000 tokens consumed" produced blank faces. "Ticket Resolved $45" made people lean forward. That single observation shaped the entire information architecture of the product.

"The founding team spoke in infrastructure. Customers spoke in outcomes. My role was to design the space between those two languages and make it feel like one product."

Because I was the only designer across both the MVP and the full platform vision, every decision I made either created coherence or fragmentation. I chose early to anchor everything to the same loop and the same vocabulary, so that nothing built for the MVP would feel misaligned when the larger platform shipped. That structural decision saved significant rework later and gave the engineering team a stable foundation to build against.

Facilitation

I ran working sessions with the founders to surface assumptions, align on what the MVP needed to prove, and separate product vision from near-term scope.

Customer proximity

I joined calls with early customers and prospects directly, not through a researcher. Hearing objections firsthand shaped decisions faster than any handoff would have.

Framing

I defined the core vocabulary of the product: what an outcome was, how value was attributed, what a delivery report meant. Language that the team adopted and used in sales conversations.

Parallel scopes

Running MVP and full platform in parallel required constant decisions about what to detail now and what to hold. I owned that prioritisation end to end.

05
Scope & prioritisation
What to build first,
and why.

Two scopes, one design language,
one product loop.

The MVP had a single constraint: everything in it had to be something a team of two could ship in weeks. That discipline forced hard prioritisation and produced a much clearer product. The large product vision extended the same loop into a full platform, with identical design system across both.

MVP ship in weeks
  • Workflow definition: name, customer, outcome, value estimate
  • Execution list with status, cost, and value per run
  • Evidence block per execution exportable as PDF
  • Value view: total generated, recent value events
  • Billing table with draft bill creation
  • Delivery report: outcomes, value, linked evidence
Full platform the vision
  • Home dashboard: revenue trends, value summary, customer table
  • Full workflow builder with block library
  • Executions with chart, trace, logs and evidence tabs
  • Value intelligence: by workflow, by customer, over time
  • Billing lifecycle: draft, review, approved, exported
  • Reports hub with automated insights

08
Outcome
What the design work
actually changed.

A clearer product that was easier
to sell, adopt, and build on.

The design work shaped how Paid.ai's team thought about and explained their own product. By making the value loop explicit and designing every screen to reinforce it, the MVP became something operators could understand immediately and customers could evaluate confidently.

The most important outcome wasn't a feature it was a mental model. Operators who used to describe their AI activity in technical terms started describing it in business outcomes. That shift, embedded in the product, made the difference between a tool people tolerated and one they could sell to a customer.

09
Learnings

What designing a new
category teaches you.

01
The mental model is the product
In an established category, users arrive with a mental model. In a new category, you design both the interface and the concept. The loop Define, Execute, Attribute, Bill, Prove was not just a UX pattern. Every screen had to reinforce it.
02
Abstract concepts need concrete anchors
The atomic unit of the whole product was "Ticket Resolved $45", not a token count. Clarity at the atomic level made everything else legible.
03
Scope discipline is a design skill
The MVP wasn't a simplified version of the big product it was a separate deliverable with its own constraint: something two engineers could ship in weeks. That discipline produced a better MVP and a more credible vision.
