01 Context

A platform with five products
and no clear north star

A portfolio with three products
and no clear north star

Factorial is an all-in-one HR platform competing in a crowded European market. When I joined as Head of Product & Design, I inherited a portfolio of five products — Time Tracking, Time Off, Scheduling, Project Management, and Space Management — each built with its own logic, its own design patterns, and its own team.

The products worked in isolation. Managers had to jump between tools to get a full picture of their workforce. HR admins were reconciling data manually. And Payroll — the product where Factorial's core revenue sat — was largely disconnected from the time data that should have been feeding it.

"The data to run payroll existed — it just lived in five different places and reached the finance team through emails and spreadsheets."

My mandate was clear: build a coherent product strategy, prioritize ruthlessly, and make Factorial's HR suite feel like one platform — not a bundle of tools. I decided to focus on three: Time Tracking, Time Off, and their connection to Payroll.

02 — The problem

Time was the lever.
Nobody had pulled it yet.

After mapping the business and talking to customers, two things became obvious. First, Time Tracking and Time Off were the highest-frequency touchpoints in the platform — employees used them daily, managers reviewed them weekly. Second, that usage wasn't converting into ecosystem value: the data generated wasn't flowing downstream into Payroll.

For employees

Clocking in and out, requesting time off, checking balances — all critical tasks with fragmented, inconsistent experiences across products.

For managers

Approving timesheets and absences was manual, error-prone, and disconnected. No single view of a team's status.

For HR admins

Payroll preparation meant exporting data, running calculations in Excel, and importing results back — every single month.

For the business

Time Tracking had strong adoption potential but wasn't driving Payroll upsell. The ecosystem value wasn't visible to customers.

The opportunity wasn't just to improve each product. It was to create a closed loop: time worked → managed → approved → paid. That loop, if we could close it, would be the strongest retention driver and growth argument in the platform.

03 — Time Tracking

Making time data
actionable for managers

The Time Tracking product needed to do two things well: give managers a clear, accurate view of their team's hours, and make the approval process fast enough that it actually got done.

We redesigned the manager timesheet view around three core concepts — estimated, worked, and balance — making it possible to spot anomalies at a glance without opening individual records.

Manager view: monthly timesheet with estimated, worked, and balance columns. Colour-coded hour distribution bars surface anomalies instantly.

Each column expands on hover: estimated shows regular + approved overtime; worked shows effective vs tracked time; balance shows running total since period start.

We also built Scheduling as a companion to Time Tracking — giving managers the ability to plan shifts in advance, publish them to employees, and use that planned time as the baseline for variance detection.

Scheduling: weekly view with shift blocks per employee. Shifts feed directly into the estimated hours calculation in Time Tracking.

04 — Time Off

Simplifying absence management
for complex organisations

Time Off was the most policy-heavy product in the portfolio. Companies needed to configure different absence types, blocked periods, approval chains, and allowances — and they needed employees to be able to request time off without calling HR.

We rebuilt the admin experience around a clear configuration model: policies first, then blocked periods, then absence types. This reduced setup time and made it obvious what rules applied to which employees.

Time Off management dashboard: pending requests with overdue alerts, absence distribution by type, weekly trend, and full employee list.

Left: policy configuration with absence types and blocked periods. Right: approval modal with multi-step workflow support — managers can approve a step or the entire request.

05 — Closing the loop with Payroll

From time worked
to payroll, automatically

The most strategically important work was invisible to most users: connecting the time data generated in Time Tracking and Time Off directly into the Payroll preparation flow.

We designed the overtime compensation flow as the first explicit bridge. When a manager approved overtime in a timesheet, they could immediately choose how to compensate it — as a payroll supplement or as additional time off. That decision flowed directly into the payroll period, eliminating the manual step that had existed before.

This was the first time in Factorial that a decision made in one product automatically populated another. It was a proof of concept for the larger ecosystem vision — and a direct driver of Payroll adoption among Time Tracking customers.

06 — Learnings

What I'd tell myself
at the start of this

  • 1

    Focus beats coverage

    I managed five products, but the real impact came from deciding which three to bet on. Time Tracking, Time Off, and Payroll had the frequency and the revenue connection. Spreading effort evenly across all five would have produced mediocre results across the board.

  • 2

    Integration is a product decision, not an engineering one

    The payroll connection only happened because we framed it as a product problem: "How does a manager decide what to do with overtime?" — not as a technical task. Starting from the user decision made the right architecture obvious.

  • 3

    Consistency compounds

    Small design decisions — the same tooltip pattern, the same approval flow, the same data table conventions — accumulated into something customers noticed without being able to articulate it. "It feels like one product" was the best feedback we got.
