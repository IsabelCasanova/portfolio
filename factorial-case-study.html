Context
Three early-stage products.
One strategic opportunity.

Three early-stage products.
One strategic opportunity.

Factorial is an all-in-one HR platform competing in a crowded European market. When I joined as Head of Product and Design, I inherited three products that were functional but early: Time Tracking, Time Off, and Scheduling. Each had been built by separate teams, with its own logic and design language.

In the HR software market, Payroll is the highest-value product in the category. Factorial had a Payroll product, but it was largely manual HR admins had to export time data, reconcile it in spreadsheets, and re-import results every single cycle.

"The data to run payroll existed. It just lived in three different products and reached the finance team through emails and spreadsheets."

My mandate: grow the three core products into something genuinely useful, then connect them to Payroll. Grow first. Connect second.

02
The challenge
Products that worked in isolation
for users who needed them together.

Products that worked in isolation for users who needed them together.

For employees

Clocking in, requesting time off, checking balances: inconsistent, underdeveloped. The basics weren't reliable enough to trust.

For managers

Reviewing timesheets and approving absences required jumping between views with no unified picture. Errors were common.

For HR admins

Closing payroll meant exporting time data, calculating in Excel, and importing back. Days of work, risk every cycle.

For the business

Time management was growing but not converting into Payroll adoption. Products weren't creating pull toward the higher-value part of the platform.

The central design decision: make time data legible at a glance. Three concepts estimated, worked, balance. No more opening individual records to spot a problem.

Manager view: monthly timesheet with estimated, worked, and balance per employee. Colour-coded bars make anomalies visible at a glance.

Each column expands on hover with detailed breakdowns.

05
Scheduling
Shifts as the input
that makes everything else work.

Planning and tracking
as one continuous flow.

Before, Scheduling lived as a separate product with no connection to what happened in Time Tracking. Managers planned shifts in one place and tracked time in another. The disconnect was constant expected hours had to be entered manually, which meant reconciliation errors before a single minute of actual overtime was calculated.

The decision was to rebuild Scheduling as the upstream input for Time Tracking, not a standalone tool. When a manager published a shift grid, those planned hours automatically populated the "estimated" column in the timesheet. Planning became the source of truth. Tracking became the measurement against it.

Scheduling: weekly shift grid per employee. Published shifts feed directly into the estimated hours calculation in Time Tracking no manual entry, no reconciliation step.

This was also the first integration we built between products. It proved the technical architecture worked and set the pattern for the Payroll connection that followed.

08
Outcome
What the work
actually moved.

From three separate products
to a platform people relied on.

Over two years, Time Tracking went from a basic clock-in tool to the manager's primary source of truth. Time Off went from a simple request form to a configurable policy engine that HR admins could own independently. Scheduling became the upstream input that made everything else work.

When we connected them to Payroll, the impact was immediate. Admins who used to spend three to five days reconciling spreadsheets every month could close payroll in a fraction of the time. The Excel step disappeared. The risk disappeared with it.

09
Learnings

What I'd do differently
and what I'd do the same.

01
Mature before you connect
Getting Time Tracking and Time Off to a level customers genuinely relied on was what made the Payroll connection meaningful. An integration built on weak products just creates a more complex version of the same problem.
02
Integration is a product decision, not an engineering one
The payroll connection only worked because we started from a user question: "What does a manager need to decide when they approve overtime?" That framing made the right solution obvious.
03
Consistency is a growth lever
Investing in shared patterns the same approval flow, the same table conventions paid off. The best feedback we got was: "It feels like one product now."
