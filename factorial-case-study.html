01 Context

Three early-stage products.
One strategic opportunity.

Factorial is an all-in-one HR platform competing in a crowded European market. When I joined as Head of Product and Design, I inherited three products that were functional but early: Time Tracking, Time Off, and Scheduling. Each had been built by separate teams, with its own logic and design language. They worked, but only just. Customers used them because they had to, not because they were good.

The strategic picture was equally underdeveloped. Factorial's revenue came primarily from time management subscriptions, and while that base was growing, it had a ceiling. In the HR software market, Payroll is the highest-value product in the category. Every company runs payroll every month, without exception. It drives the deepest retention and the highest contract value. Factorial had a Payroll product, but it was largely manual, requiring HR admins to export time data, reconcile it in spreadsheets, and re-import results every single cycle.

"The data to run payroll existed. It just lived in three different products and reached the finance team through emails and spreadsheets."

My mandate was to grow the three core products into something genuinely useful, and then connect them to Payroll to unlock the platform's real potential. Grow first. Connect second.

02 The challenge

Products that worked in isolation
for users who needed them together.

Talking to customers made the gaps clear. Each product had its own problems, and they compounded each other. Fixing them individually would help. But the real opportunity was in what happened between them.

For employees

Clocking in, requesting time off, checking balances: all high-frequency tasks with inconsistent, underdeveloped experiences. The basics weren't reliable enough to trust.

For managers

Reviewing timesheets and approving absences required jumping between views with no unified picture of the team. Errors were common and hard to catch.

For HR admins

Closing payroll every month meant manually exporting time data, calculating supplements in Excel, and importing everything back. A process that took days and introduced risk every cycle.

For the business

Time management was growing but not converting into Payroll adoption. The products weren't creating pull toward the higher-value part of the platform.

The path forward had two phases: mature each product to a level where customers actually relied on it, then build the connections that would make switching away from Factorial genuinely painful.

03 How I worked

Decisions made close
to customers and data.

My process across all three products followed the same rhythm. I started by talking directly to customers — through Customer Experience, Sales, and direct interviews — to understand where the real friction was. Not the friction they described first, but the friction underneath it: the workaround they'd stopped noticing, the task they'd given up trying to do in the product.

I combined that with usage data to prioritise. Customer feedback tells you what hurts; data tells you how often and for how many. Together they made it possible to argue for the right bets in a team of 50 people with competing priorities.

Customer interviews

Direct sessions with HR managers, admins, and employees, coordinated with CX and Sales to surface real pain — not just feature requests.

Data analysis

Usage patterns, drop-off points, and adoption curves to validate what customers said and prioritise by actual impact.

Team workshops

Cross-functional sessions with the product and engineering teams to align on the problem before touching solutions. I facilitated, challenged, and pushed for clarity.

Co-design and critique

I was hands-on throughout. I sketched alongside the designers, ran critiques, defined interaction patterns, and made the key design calls myself. Leading the team didn't mean stepping back from the work — it meant being the person who raised the bar on it.

I operated as a player-coach: setting direction and removing blockers for the team, while staying hands-on in the design work itself. I didn't delegate the hard design problems — I worked through them with the team, and often drove the solution.

One of the first things I mapped was the HR admin's payroll closing process as it existed before we touched it. This is what surfaced the Excel reconciliation step as the most critical problem — and ultimately defined the product direction.

As-is process map: the HR admin's monthly payroll closing before the solution. Mapped from customer interviews and CX sessions. The Excel reconciliation step — highlighted — was the single biggest source of errors and time loss.

04 Time Tracking

From basic clock-in tool
to the manager's source of truth.

When I joined, Time Tracking let employees log hours. That was about it. Managers had no real visibility, approval was cumbersome, and the data generated was barely usable. Customer interviews confirmed what the data showed: managers weren't using it to manage — they were working around it.

I made the call to rebuild it as a management tool, not just an employee one. The central design decision — which I developed with the team through workshops and co-design — was to make time data legible at a glance. Three concepts: what was expected (estimated), what actually happened (worked), and the difference between them (balance). No more opening individual records to spot a problem.

Manager view: monthly timesheet with estimated, worked, and balance per employee. Colour-coded hour distribution bars make anomalies visible at a glance.

Each column expands on hover: estimated breaks down regular hours and approved overtime; worked separates effective time from tracked time; balance shows the running total since the period started.

I also pushed for Scheduling to be rebuilt as a proper companion, not a separate product. Instead of treating shifts as a separate concern, we made them the input that Time Tracking used to calculate what was expected. Planning and tracking became one continuous flow.

Scheduling: weekly shift grid per employee, published directly from the manager view. Planned shifts feed the estimated hours calculation in Time Tracking automatically.

05 Time Off

From a request form
to a policy engine.

Time Off had the same problem as Time Tracking: it was built for a simple case and fell apart under real-world complexity. Companies have different absence types, different approval chains, different rules for different teams. The original product couldn't handle any of that. HR admins were managing the gaps manually.

We rebuilt the admin experience around a clear configuration model. Policies came first, defining the rules. Blocked periods came next, restricting availability. Absence types came last, giving employees the right vocabulary to make requests. Setup that used to take a support call could now be done independently.

Dashboard: pending requests with overdue alerts, absence breakdown by type, weekly trend across offices, and full employee log.

Left: policy configuration with absence types, blocked periods and approval chains. Right: approval modal supporting multi-step workflows.

06 Connecting to Payroll

The loop closes:
time worked becomes payroll, automatically.

With Time Tracking and Time Off working reliably, we had the foundation we needed for the second phase: connecting them to Payroll. This was the strategic move that transformed three standalone products into a platform.

In the HR software market, payroll is where companies commit. It's the highest-stakes, highest-frequency operation HR runs. If we could make payroll preparation flow directly from the time data already in the system, we would eliminate the monthly manual reconciliation that every HR admin dreaded, and make Factorial genuinely hard to leave.

The first bridge we built was overtime compensation. When a manager approved extra hours in a timesheet, they could immediately decide how to handle them: pay the employee directly via a payroll supplement, or convert the hours into additional time off. That single decision, made in Time Tracking, flowed automatically into the active payroll period.

07 Learnings

What I'd do differently
and what I'd do the same.

  • 1

    Mature before you connect

    The temptation early on was to build the Payroll integration before the upstream products were solid. I'm glad we resisted. An integration built on top of weak products just creates a more complex version of the same problem. Getting Time Tracking and Time Off to a level customers genuinely relied on was what made the connection meaningful.

  • 2

    Integration is a product decision, not an engineering one

    The payroll connection only worked because we started from a user question: "What does a manager need to decide when they approve overtime?" That framing made the right solution obvious. Starting from the technical question would have produced a different, worse answer.

  • 3

    Consistency is a growth lever

    As we matured each product, we invested heavily in shared patterns: the same approval flow, the same tooltip system, the same table conventions. Customers noticed without being able to articulate it. The best feedback we got was simply: "It feels like one product now." That coherence was a meaningful part of why adoption grew.
